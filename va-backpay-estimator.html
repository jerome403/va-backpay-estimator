The VA starts with the highest-rated disability. Then for each subsequent disability, it applies that percentage to the remaining healthy portion. For example, with two 50% disabilities: 50% + (50% × 50%) = 75%, rounded to 80%.

The final combined value is rounded to the nearest 10%. This means 75% rounds up to 80%, but 74% rounds down to 70%.

Bilateral factor: When you have disabilities affecting both sides of the body (both arms, both legs, etc.), the VA applies a "bilateral factor." The bilateral disabilities are combined separately, then 10% of that combined value is added. This adjusted value is then combined with your non-bilateral disabilities. For example: left knee 30% + right knee 20% = 44% combined, bilateral factor adds 4.4%, giving 48.4% before combining with other disabilities.

0% ratings: A 0% service-connected rating doesn't change the combined math, but it establishes service connection which can be important for future claims and VA benefits eligibility.

SMC-K ($139.87/mo in 2026) — Loss of use of a creative organ, hand, foot, eye, or both ears. Stacks on top of any other level (max 3 awards).

SMC-S — Housebound. Either one disability at 100% plus separate disabilities at 60%+, or factually confined to dwelling.

SMC-L — Aid & Attendance or bilateral loss. Loss of both feet, one hand + one foot, bilateral blindness (5/200 or less), permanently bedridden, or needing regular A&A.

SMC-M — Loss of both hands, both legs above knee, one arm + one leg (preventing prosthetic use), or bilateral blindness with only light perception.

SMC-N — Both arms above elbow, both legs near hip, or anatomical loss of both eyes.

SMC-O — Both arms near shoulder, two separate conditions each qualifying for L through N, or paraplegia with sphincter loss. Also reached via bump-ups capped at O.

SMC-R.1 — At O level and needs regular aid & attendance (non-professional care).

SMC-R.2 — At O level and needs A&A from a licensed healthcare professional.

SMC-T — TBI requiring A&A, would need institutional care, not eligible for R.2. Same rate as R.2.

Bump-ups (38 CFR 3.350): Additional separate disabilities combining to 50%+ = half-step bump. A single separate 100% disability = full-step bump. Per Barry v. McDonough (2024), multiple bumps are allowed, capped at SMC-O.

Half-step levels (L½, M½, N½) are intermediate rates between full levels, reached via bump-up rules.

What-If Comparison

Compare two rating scenarios side-by-side to see the difference in total monthly compensation over a date range. Uses dependent info from the Backpay Calculator tab if set.

Effective date: When VA determines your disability began or when you filed your claim. This is the start of your backpay period.

Calculation method: VA pays the monthly rate for each month between effective date and decision. If your rating increased, you receive the difference between old and new rates. Rates change annually on December 1st due to COLA adjustments.

Partial months: The month after the effective date is the first payment month. Partial months are generally counted as full months.

Staged ratings: Some claims result in different ratings for different time periods. The VA assigns the rating that best reflects your disability level at each point in time.

Payment: Backpay is usually a lump sum shortly after your claim decision.
